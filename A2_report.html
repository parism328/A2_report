Assignment 2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Paris Myers — parismyers@mit.edu

Subtheme: Speculation

Dataset: Condo Conversions in the City of Boston, 2015–2024. I decided on the Speculation housing theme — specifically the reading When Private Equity Becomes Your Landlord. Based on the reading and my own housing experience in Cambridge, I was particularly interested in the Condo Conversions dataset for Boston and Cambridge as it seemed the most "embodied" or immediately personal to me (i.e. you could visually see in your neighborhood or in your home the effects of new ownership).

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How do condo conversions change the physical aesthetic of a neighborhood — e.g. its colors, textures, materials, and scale?
  2. How does visualizing real-world image data (via Google Maps) influence perception of (and connection to) before-and-after changes?
  3. How is using Claude for data visualization different from using an existing/canon visualization tool? Does it introduce a new kind of bias?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

As someone who loves to draw and paint things physically, I find digital data analysis tools to be both restrictive and expressive. What I distinctly love about digital data visualization is the possibility for interactivity — zooming in and out — and the inclusion of exact photographic or geographic data. It was extremely interesting to run into the limitations of different softwares in the effort to answer my initial question, which I thought was straightforward.

I went into this being more interested in the literal housing conversions, but when I started really seeing what was in these I became more interested in the underlying price driver: land! I came into working on this data set assuming I was dealing with housing buildings, probably under 5 million tops. When I graphed the land value, I was blown away by these numbers and assumed there must be errors. In the end, I became curious about if there is a relationship between where land becomes expensive and where the population is more or less migrating in an area.

I would love to call this a Data Journal. I found that in my efforts to explore or see the data in the way I wanted, I ended up actually exploring different tools and how to connect those tools. Perhaps my exploration of the data itself is limited — but I feel that my attention keeps being drawn, both out of necessity and intrigue, to the larger forces at play that govern our access to and presentation of data.

My final closing question is: how will AI-assisted graphs of real data shape the narratives of our reality? This is a wildly different question than the one I started with — and I think that is the point.
